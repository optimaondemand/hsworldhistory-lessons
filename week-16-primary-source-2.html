Thomas More, Utopia
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Written in 1516, before Luther's Theses and nearly two decades before More's own execution, Utopia critiques contemporary English social problems through the device of an imagined ideal island society, described to the narrator by a fictional traveler.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Utopia, Book I, on enclosure (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Gilbert Burnet, 1684 (public domain). Excerpt.
“Your sheep, that were wont to be so meek and tame, and so small eaters, now… become so great devourers, and so wild, that they eat up and swallow down the very men themselves… the noblemen and gentlemen… leave no ground for tillage, they enclose all into pastures; they throw down houses; they pluck down towns, and leave nothing standing but only the church, to make it a sheep-house… by one means, therefore, or by other, either by hook or crook, they must needs depart away, poor, silly, wretched souls.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

More's vivid image of sheep “eating men” is a satirical exaggeration making a real economic point: converting farmland to sheep pasture for wool profit was displacing poor tenant farmers on a genuinely large scale in early Tudor England.
