📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
By 1400, an empire that had once ruled from Spain to Persia was reduced to a single, magnificent, doomed city.

The Byzantine Empire's final decades were a story of desperate diplomacy against overwhelming odds. Byzantine emperors repeatedly traveled to the West in person, an extraordinary and humbling step, to beg for military aid, even offering to reunite the Orthodox and Catholic churches (which had been divided since the Great Schism of 1054 you first met in Week 9) in exchange for help. Western Europe's response was thin and inconsistent: a crusading army was raised in 1444 and marched to relieve the pressure, but it was crushed at the Battle of Varna, and no comparable effort followed.

📚 PART 1 · WHY THE WEST DID NOT COME

Why did the West, which had once mobilized tens of thousands of knights for the First Crusade, do so little for Byzantium at its moment of greatest need? Several genuine, overlapping causes deserve weight rather than a single simple explanation: deep and lingering religious distrust between Orthodox and Catholic Christians dating to 1054 and reinforced by the West's own catastrophic sack of Constantinople in 1204 (Week 9); Western Europe's own preoccupations, including the Hundred Years War you studied last week; and simple geographic distance and the practical difficulty of mounting another large expedition after the costly failure at Varna. No one of these fully explains Byzantium's isolation; together, they do.

📚 PART 2 · A CITY ALONE

By the reign of the last Byzantine emperor, Constantine XI, who came to the throne in 1449, the empire he ruled consisted of little beyond Constantinople's own walls and a handful of nearby territories. Yet Constantinople itself remained one of the most heavily fortified cities in the world, protected by the massive Theodosian land walls that had turned back besiegers for a thousand years, since their completion in the 5th century. Constantine XI would need every one of those walls' advantages, because the ruler who would soon move against the city, the young Ottoman sultan Mehmet II, was already assembling the largest siege the city had ever faced: the subject of next week's lesson.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Notice the arc: the empire that survived Rome's fall in Week 2, reconquered the Mediterranean under Justinian, weathered Justinian's Plague, the Crusades, and centuries of Islamic and Turkic pressure, now stands nearly alone against the empire that will finally end it. Keep this full thousand-year arc in mind for next week.
