📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A new confidence in what a single human mind and a single pair of hands could achieve produced, in barely a century, some of the most celebrated art and thought in Western history.

The intellectual movement historians call humanism, whose earliest and most influential figure was the 14th-century poet and scholar Petrarch, centered on the close study of classical Greek and Roman texts, not primarily as sources of theology (though humanists were almost all devout Christians), but as models of eloquence, ethics, and human achievement in their own right. This did not mean rejecting Christian faith, as it would be anachronistic and inaccurate to claim; it meant a shift in emphasis toward human potential, individual accomplishment, and the dignity of reasoned inquiry alongside continued religious devotion: recall this same combination, of classical inheritance serving religious ends, from the Carolingian Renaissance in Module 3, now developing in a more secular, individual-centered direction.

📚 PART 1 · RENAISSANCE HUMANISM IN ART

Standard W.4.4 asks you to identify specific characteristics of this humanism as it appears in visual art: a renewed interest in accurate human anatomy and naturalistic proportion (rather than the more symbolic, less physically precise figures typical of medieval religious art), the mathematical technique of linear perspective (pioneered by the architect Filippo Brunelleschi, whose dome for Florence's cathedral, completed in 1436 without any medieval-style external scaffolding, was itself considered a near-miraculous feat of engineering), and a revival of classical subject matter and poses even within religious paintings.

Sandro Botticelli's mythological paintings, Donatello's freestanding sculptures (the first since antiquity), and, slightly later, the work of Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo, all built on this foundation of naturalism, classical influence, and mathematical technique. Standard W.4.3 asks you to recognize such figures as making major, identifiable, individual contributions: a genuinely new emphasis on the celebrated individual artist as a figure of accomplishment in his own right, a further mark of humanism's influence, in contrast to the largely anonymous craftsmen who built most medieval cathedrals.

📚 PART 2 · MACHIAVELLI AND THE HISTORY OF FLORENCE

Humanism also transformed how history and politics themselves were studied. Niccolò Machiavelli, a Florentine diplomat and political theorist writing in the early 16th century, brought a sharp, unsentimental, empirically-minded eye to Florence's own turbulent political history in his Istorie Fiorentine (History of Florence), which you will read today: the same clear-eyed, virtue-and-vice-attentive approach to political history, incidentally, that later inspired the Classical Republican historiography this course itself draws on. Machiavelli's willingness to describe political actors' real motives and failures, rather than idealizing them, is itself a product of humanism's confidence that human affairs could be studied and understood rationally, on their own terms, rather than only through providential or moralizing explanation.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

The Renaissance's confidence in human reason, individual achievement, and careful observation of the natural and political world did not stay confined to art and history. As you will see in the coming modules, this same confidence would soon be turned toward religious authority itself, and then toward the natural world in the Scientific Revolution.
