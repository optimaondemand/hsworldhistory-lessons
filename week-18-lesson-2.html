Westphalia and the End of an Idea

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In 1648, the diplomats of Europe did more than end a war. They quietly buried an idea this entire course has followed since Charlemagne: one unified Christian empire under a single emperor and pope.

After thirty years of nearly continuous warfare, exhaustion on every side finally produced the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, actually a set of treaties negotiated simultaneously at Münster and Osnabrück. Westphalia extended the Peace of Augsburg's religious settlement to formally include Calvinism alongside Catholicism and Lutheranism, closing the specific doctrinal gap that had helped ignite the war in 1618.

📚 PART 1 · THE SOVEREIGN STATE

Westphalia's deeper and more lasting achievement was political rather than religious: it effectively recognized the individual states of the Holy Roman Empire, and by extension the states of Europe generally, as sovereign, independent political units, free to conduct their own foreign policy and manage their own internal religious affairs without outside interference: the foundational principle of what historians and political scientists still call the Westphalian system, the basic framework of independent, sovereign nation-states that underlies international law and diplomacy to this day.

Consider what this meant for the Holy Roman Empire itself, and for an idea you have followed since Week 5: Charlemagne's coronation in 800 as a Christian Roman Emperor, revived by Otto I in 962, contested through the Investiture Controversy in Module 4, was never formally abolished by Westphalia (the empire limped on in name until 1806) but its practical claim to be one unified political-religious authority over Western Christendom was now definitively hollow. The empire became, in practice, a loose confederation of hundreds of largely autonomous princely states. The medieval dream of one Christian empire under one emperor and one church, alive in some form for eight and a half centuries, quietly ended not with a dramatic final battle, but with the stroke of diplomatic pens at a peace conference.

📚 PART 2 · WHERE THIS COURSE ENDS, AND WHERE HISTORY DOES NOT

This course closes here, at 1648, but the paths it has traced do not. France's wartime centralization under Richelieu would reach its fullest expression under Louis XIV's absolutism in the coming decades. England's Parliamentary resistance to royal war-taxation, already visible in Charles I's conflicts over ship money, erupted into full civil war in 1642 and would eventually produce, after more upheaval than this course has space to cover, a constitutional monarchy in which Parliament's power was permanently secured. You will follow both of these stories, along with the Enlightenment ideas that would help justify them, in your Spring semester.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS: A FALL SEMESTER IN REVIEW

Look back across the full eighteen weeks of this course: an empire that fell in the West and endured for a thousand years in the East; peoples who built new kingdoms out of Rome's wreckage; a faith that reshaped half the known world; a Carolingian revival, a feudal order, a church-state conflict without a clean winner; a holy war launched to save an empire that Western Christians later sacked themselves; Mongols who conquered nearly everything except one island; a Renaissance sparked partly by a falling empire's fleeing scholars; a religious unity shattered permanently in a single generation; and a war so costly it helped invent the modern state. If there is one thread running through all of it, this course has argued, it is not a single law of progress or decline, but contingency: particular people, making particular choices, at moments that could very often have gone differently. Your Fall Semester Capstone asks you to defend that claim, or challenge it, with the full weight of everything you have studied.
