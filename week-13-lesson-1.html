1453: The Fall of Constantinople
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
On 29 May 1453, after fifty-three days of siege, the walls that had held for a thousand years finally broke. The last Roman emperor died fighting in the breach.

The young Ottoman sultan Mehmet II, determined to succeed where every previous besieger had failed, prepared with extraordinary care. He commissioned a Hungarian engineer, Orban, to cast some of the largest cannon yet built, capable of battering even Constantinople's massive Theodosian walls. When the Byzantines stretched a great chain across the Golden Horn to block Ottoman ships from the harbor, Mehmet had dozens of ships hauled overland on greased logs to bypass the chain entirely: a feat of engineering audacity that stunned defenders and attackers alike.

📚 PART 1 · FIFTY-THREE DAYS

Constantine XI commanded a defense force of perhaps 7,000 against an Ottoman army numbering in the tens of thousands, relying on the city's ancient walls and a small number of Genoese and Venetian volunteers, most notably the soldier Giovanni Giustiniani. For weeks, the defense held. On 29 May 1453, following weeks of continuous bombardment, Ottoman forces finally breached the walls near the Gate of Saint Romanus. Constantine XI, according to tradition, tore off his imperial insignia and threw himself into the final fighting rather than survive the city's fall; his body was never conclusively identified afterward. Mehmet II entered the city and famously rode to the Hagia Sophia (the church Justinian built, which you visited in your Week 2 VR activity) and converted it into a mosque.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Constantinople's walls had turned back besiegers for a thousand years. What finally changed to make 1453 different?
📚 PART 2 · THE END OF ROME

Take a moment to register the scale of what ended in 1453. The line of Roman emperors, stretching back through Justinian (Week 2), through Constantine's founding of Constantinople in 330, through Augustus in 27 BC, had continued in some form for nearly 1,500 years. It ended, finally and completely, with Constantine XI in the streets of his own capital. Mehmet II, who took the additional title “Caesar of Rome” for himself, understood exactly what he had accomplished, whatever the West's reluctance to grant an Ottoman sultan that Roman inheritance.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

The fall of Constantinople is one of the great hinge-points of world history, closing the ancient world's last living political link to Rome. It is also, as tomorrow's lesson shows, a beginning: the flight of Greek scholars and their books westward, in the years just before and after 1453, would help ignite one of the West's greatest intellectual flowerings.
