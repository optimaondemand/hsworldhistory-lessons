💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Doukas was a Byzantine Greek historian writing in the mid-15th century, a firsthand witness to the empire's final decades, whose history is one of our most valuable sources for the Ottoman rise and Byzantium's collapse.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Decline and Fall of Byzantium to the Ottoman Turks (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Harry J. Magoulias, 1975 (translation of a public-domain-era Greek text); excerpt.
“The queen of cities [Constantinople] was now like a body encircled and pressed upon on every side, having lost almost all her limbs, and nothing remained to her save her head alone… And the emperor sent to the princes and lords of the West, beseeching aid, but they, occupied with their own wars and troubles, gave little heed… for they said among themselves, ‘What is Constantinople to us, so far away?’ not perceiving that when the head is cut off, the body cannot long survive.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Doukas's image, “when the head is cut off, the body cannot long survive,” is a warning aimed squarely at the West: that Constantinople's fall would matter to all of Christendom, not just to the Byzantines themselves. Weigh how right or wrong this prediction turned out to be as you complete this week's VR Activity.
