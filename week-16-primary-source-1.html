Dudley's Tree of Commonwealth
Week 16 · Primary Source 1
Edmund Dudley, The Tree of Commonwealth
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Edmund Dudley, a councilor to Henry VIII's father, Henry VII, wrote this treatise on good governance in 1509–1510 while imprisoned awaiting his own execution, ordered by the young Henry VIII partly to distance himself from his father's unpopular financial policies. Dudley's tree metaphor describes the roots a healthy commonwealth requires.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Tree of Commonwealth (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Ed. D. M. Brodie, 1948 (modernized-spelling edition of a public-domain-era English text); excerpt.
“This tree of commonwealth must be principally maintained by five roots… the first is the love of God… the second is true and diligent administration of justice… the third is concord and unity of the several estates and degrees of the same commonwealth… the fourth root is peace… and the fifth root is the plenty and prosperity of the poor commons of the same commonwealth.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Dudley's own execution shortly after writing this treatise, ordered by the very king he hoped might read it, is a bitter irony worth sitting with: a man describing the ideal foundations of good government while himself a victim of its arbitrary exercise.
