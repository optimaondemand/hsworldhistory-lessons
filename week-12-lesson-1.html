The Rise of the Ottoman Turks

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A small Anatolian frontier principality grew, within two centuries, into an empire that would encircle and finally swallow the empire you have followed since Week 2.

Around 1299, a Turkish frontier chieftain named Osman I founded a small principality (beylik) in northwestern Anatolia, on Byzantium's exposed eastern frontier. His successors, the Ottoman dynasty named for him, expanded steadily at Byzantine expense, crossing into Europe for the first time in 1354 and advancing rapidly through the Balkans, crushing a coalition of Balkan Christian states at the Battle of Kosovo in 1389.

📚 PART 1 · THE JANISSARIES

Central to Ottoman military success was an institution called the devshirme, under which the sultan's officials periodically levied a tribute of Christian boys from conquered Balkan territories, converted them to Islam, and trained them from childhood into an elite corps of professional soldiers personally loyal to the sultan alone: the Janissaries. Because Janissaries owed their position entirely to the sultan rather than to inherited noble status or tribal loyalty, they functioned as a genuinely meritocratic force free of the competing aristocratic loyalties that could weaken a feudal army: a system with real echoes of the Mongol meritocratic promotion you studied last week, achieving a similar practical result (a disciplined force loyal to the ruler alone) through a very different, and for the families involved, deeply painful mechanism.

QUICK CHECK-IN
The Janissary system took Christian boys from conquered territory and made them the sultan's most loyal soldiers. What historical judgment does this system require you to hold together?
📚 PART 2 · A SHRINKING EMPIRE

By the early 15th century, Ottoman territory nearly surrounded what remained of the Byzantine Empire, which by this point had shrunk to little more than the city of Constantinople itself and a few scattered outposts, a shadow of the empire that had reconquered Italy and North Africa under Justinian (Week 2) or that had once stretched deep into Anatolia. Standard W.2.7's list of causes for Byzantine decline (Justinian's Plague, centuries of external pressure, the Crusades' devastating 1204 sack of Constantinople itself (Week 9), and now sustained internal political weakness compounding continuous Ottoman pressure) is not a single cause but an accumulation across nearly a thousand years, exactly the kind of layered, contingent causation this course has asked you to trace since Module 1.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Byzantium's pleas for Western military help, much like Alexios I's appeal that helped launch the First Crusade three centuries earlier, went largely unanswered this time, or arrived too late and in too small a force, as tomorrow's lesson on the empire's final years will show.
