💡 BEFORE YOU READ

The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle is a year-by-year record of English history, begun under Alfred's patronage in the 890s and continued by later scribes for centuries afterward. This entry describes the campaign leading to the Battle of Edington in 878.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, entry for the year 878 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. James Ingram, 1823 (public domain). Excerpt.
“This year, in mid-winter, after twelfth-night, the [Danish] army stole away to Chippenham, and rode over the land of the West-Saxons, and there abode; and many of the people they drove over sea, and of the rest the greater part they reduced to submission… and the West-Saxons could no longer withstand them. At Easter, King Alfred, with a small band, built a fortress at Athelney, and from that fastness continued fighting against the enemy… Then, in the seventh week after Easter, he rode to Egbert's stone… and there came to meet him all the men of Somerset, and Wiltshire, and Hampshire… and they fought with the entire army, and put it to flight… And the king remained there fourteen nights, when the army delivered up hostages to him with many oaths, and promised him also that their king would receive baptism.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice how close this account puts Alfred to total defeat (hiding at Athelney “with a small band”) before the counties rally to him. This detail matters for how you evaluate the “Great” epithet: it was earned from the brink of collapse, not from a position of strength.
