Feudalism: Lords, Vassals, and Fiefs
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When Charlemagne's empire broke apart, Western Europe had no central government strong enough to protect its people. Feudalism was the answer people actually built, piece by piece, out of necessity.

Charlemagne's empire did not survive his grandsons. In 843, the Treaty of Verdun formally split it into three kingdoms among his three grandsons, roughly corresponding to the future France, Germany, and a middle strip contested between them for centuries afterward. Almost immediately, these weakened, divided kingdoms faced a new wave of outside raiding: Vikings from Scandinavia along every navigable river and coastline, Magyars sweeping in from the east, and Muslim raiders along the Mediterranean coast.

📚 PART 1 · A PRACTICAL RESPONSE TO WEAK GOVERNMENT

A distant king in this environment could rarely muster and move an army fast enough to stop a Viking raid on a coastal monastery, let alone govern effectively day-to-day. Local strongmen, holding fortified positions and mounted retinues of armed warriors, became the only realistic source of protection for ordinary people. Out of this practical necessity, a set of customs, gradually formalized over the 9th through 11th centuries, that historians later labeled feudalism took shape: a great lord granted land, a fief, to a lesser noble, his vassal, in exchange for the vassal's sworn military service and loyalty. That vassal might, in turn, grant portions of his own fief to lesser vassals of his own, creating a layered pyramid of obligation running from a king or great duke down to the knight who actually held and defended a given piece of land.

Crucially, feudalism was never a single, uniform legal system applied evenly everywhere; it varied enormously by region and period, and historians today debate how coherent a single “feudal system” really was even at the time. What is not in dispute is the underlying logic: personal, sworn bonds of loyalty and service, tied to grants of land, filled the vacuum left by weak central government: a Classical Republican observer would say these societies solved their first-order political problem (defense against external threats) through informal, personal bonds rather than strong central institutions, at real cost to internal unity and the rule of law.

📚 PART 2 · PRIVATE PROPERTY AND THE MANOR

Beneath the lord-vassal relationship among the aristocracy sat the manor, the basic economic unit of medieval rural life. Most peasants, called serfs, were legally bound to the land they worked and owed labor and a portion of their harvest to their lord in exchange for his protection and the right to farm strips of land for their own use. Serfs were not slaves (they generally could not be bought, sold, or separated from their land and families the way chattel slaves could be, and they held customary, inheritable rights to their strips) but they were also not free to leave without their lord's permission.

Out of this system, an important and distinctively Western institution began to take clearer shape: enforceable, hereditary private property rights, distinguishing ownership from mere possession and creating legal claims that could be defended in a lord's court. This gradual clarification of property rights, imperfect and unevenly applied as it was, would matter enormously for the economic development you will study later this course, in the growth of towns, trade, and a market economy during the High Middle Ages.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Feudalism and manorialism were imperfect, often unjust solutions to a real problem: how to organize defense, agriculture, and loyalty without a strong central state. Keep this pattern (weak central authority, filled by personal bonds and local arrangements) in mind next week, when you meet the Viking raiders themselves and the kingdom, England, that would eventually build one of medieval Europe's stronger central monarchies partly in response to exactly this threat.
