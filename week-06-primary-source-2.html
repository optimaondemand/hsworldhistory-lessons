The Song of Roland
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Composed around 1100, roughly three centuries after the events it claims to describe, this Old French epic poem (chanson de geste) transforms a real, minor defeat of Charlemagne's rearguard at Roncevaux Pass in 778 into a grand, idealized story of feudal loyalty and betrayal. It reveals more about 11th-century feudal values than about the 8th-century event itself.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Song of Roland, laisses 83–84 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Charles Scott Moncrieff, 1919 (public domain). Excerpt.
“Roland is dead; God hath his soul in Heaven… Right well he knew if he his horn should sound, Charles would return with all his Franks so proud… but shame he counted the deed. ‘Nay, by God's grace,’ then answered Sir Rollant, ‘It shall not be through me my house falls down, Nor that fair France be brought to shame and doubt… ill deed were mine, if for that horn I sound.’… Oliver saith: ‘Companion, look you now, this pagan host, methinks, is very near, and, look, our Franks methinks are far away. I pray you, sound your horn of ivory, and Charles the king shall hear it, and turn back…’… Rollant replies: ‘An evil word and vain! So in fair France should I lose my good name.’”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Roland refuses, at first, to sound his horn for help because doing so would shame him: a value system built entirely around personal honor and reputation among one's lord and companions, exactly the culture of loyalty and reputation that made the feudal bond function without a strong central government enforcing it.
