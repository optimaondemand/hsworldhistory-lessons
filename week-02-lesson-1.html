New Rome in the East: Constantine, Theodosius, and the Byzantine Inheritance
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
While the West was collapsing, the Eastern half of the empire was not merely surviving. It was becoming something new: a Christian Roman empire that would outlast the West by a thousand years.

In 330, more than a century before Alaric's sack of Rome, the emperor Constantine the Great dedicated a new capital on the site of the old Greek city of Byzantium, at the crossroads between Europe and Asia. He named it Constantinople, but he and his successors continued to call it simply New Rome. This was not a retreat from Rome; it was a repositioning. Constantinople sat astride the wealthiest trade routes in the Mediterranean world and was far easier to defend and supply than Rome itself, protected on three sides by water and, in time, by the most formidable city walls of the ancient world.

📚 PART 1 · A CHRISTIAN EMPIRE

Constantine's other transformation was religious. In 313, with his co-emperor Licinius, he issued the Edict of Milan, legalizing Christianity throughout the empire after centuries of intermittent persecution. Constantine himself was baptized only on his deathbed in 337, and historians still debate how much of his own faith was genuine conviction and how much was shrewd politics: recognizing Christianity's spreading influence rather than merely deciding to adopt it. Both readings can be true at once; a Great Man does not need pure motives to change history. By 380, under Emperor Theodosius I, Christianity became not merely legal but the empire's official religion, and the old pagan temples began to close.

In 395, Theodosius divided administration of the empire between his two sons, one ruling the West from Italy, the other the East from Constantinople. This was meant as an administrative convenience, not a permanent split: but it became one. The West, as you saw last week, collapsed within a century. The East did not.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Constantinople's founders called it “New Rome.” Do you think that is an accurate description, or a piece of political branding?
📚 PART 2 · CONTINUITY AND DEPARTURE

Historians call this eastern state the Byzantine Empire, but its own citizens never used that word: they called themselves Rhomaioi, Romans, until the empire's final fall in 1453. In important ways, they were right to. Byzantine emperors kept Roman law, Roman administrative titles, and (for a long time) a functioning Senate. Roman roads, Roman taxation, and a professional, salaried army continued largely intact in the East for centuries after they had broken down in the West.

Yet real departures were underway. Latin gradually gave way to Greek as the empire's working language. The emperor's role fused political and religious authority far more tightly than in pagan Rome, with the emperor understood as God's regent on Earth. And the empire's center of gravity, culturally and economically, shifted permanently toward the Greek-speaking eastern Mediterranean. Whether you call this state “Rome continued” or “something new grown from Rome's roots” is a real historical judgment, not a settled fact: and it is one you will be asked to defend yourself.
