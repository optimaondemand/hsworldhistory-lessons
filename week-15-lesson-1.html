Forerunners and the Ninety-Five Theses
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A German monk nailed a list of academic debate points to a church door in 1517. He did not intend to split Western Christianity in two. He did anyway.

Criticism of the Roman Catholic Church's wealth, corruption, and doctrine did not begin with Martin Luther. A century and a half earlier, the English theologian John Wycliffe had criticized clerical wealth and sponsored an early English translation of the Bible; his Bohemian follower Jan Hus carried similar ideas into Central Europe and was burned at the stake as a heretic in 1415, at the very Council of Constance that resolved the Great Schism you studied in Module 6. The Christian humanist scholar Erasmus of Rotterdam, writing just before Luther, satirized clerical corruption sharply in works like In Praise of Folly while remaining within the Catholic Church himself: a later saying held that “Erasmus laid the egg that Luther hatched.”

📚 PART 1 · INDULGENCES AND THE NINETY-FIVE THESES

Martin Luther, an Augustinian monk and theology professor at Wittenberg, was troubled by the practice of selling indulgences: documents purchased from the church, in this case aggressively marketed by the friar Johann Tetzel to help fund the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, that were popularly (if not always precisely, in official doctrine) understood to reduce time in purgatory for the buyer or a deceased loved one. In 1517, Luther wrote out Ninety-Five Theses, formal points for academic debate, challenging the theology and practice behind indulgence sales, and, according to tradition, posted them to the door of Wittenberg's Castle Church, the customary place for announcing a university disputation.

What made 1517 different from Wycliffe's and Hus's earlier criticism was not necessarily the boldness of the ideas alone, but a genuinely new technology: the printing press, invented by Johannes Gutenberg around 1450. Luther's Theses, originally written in Latin for academic debate, were quickly translated into German and printed in the thousands, spreading across the German lands within weeks in a way no earlier reform movement's message ever could: a striking example, once again, of material and technological change reshaping what ideas can actually accomplish.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Wycliffe and Hus made similar criticisms of the church over a century before Luther, yet Luther's movement produced a lasting religious split while theirs did not survive to do so at the same scale. What best explains the difference?
💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Luther did not set out to found a new church; he wanted to reform practices within the existing one through academic debate. Tomorrow's lesson shows how quickly that modest goal escaped his control.
