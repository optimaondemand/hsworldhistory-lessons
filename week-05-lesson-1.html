📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
On Christmas Day, 800, a Frankish king knelt in Saint Peter's Basilica and rose an emperor. Was this the revival of Rome, or something new wearing Rome's name?

You met the Carolingian family last week as Mayors of the Palace, the officials who had quietly out-governed the Merovingian kings they nominally served. In 751, Charlemagne's father, Pepin the Short, formalized the takeover: with papal blessing, he deposed the last Merovingian king and took the crown himself, founding the Carolingian dynasty. When Pepin's son Charlemagne (“Charles the Great”) inherited sole rule in 771, he inherited a kingdom and used it to build an empire.

📚 PART 1 · A LIFETIME OF CONQUEST

Over more than four decades of nearly continuous campaigning, Charlemagne conquered the Lombard kingdom of Italy, pushed the frontier of Frankish rule into northern Spain, and, in a brutal three-decade war, subdued and forcibly Christianized the pagan Saxons of northern Germany, at one point ordering the execution of thousands of Saxon prisoners after a revolt at Verden in 782. This is not a comfortable fact, and it should not be smoothed over: Charlemagne's Christianization of conquered peoples was frequently achieved by the sword, not persuasion, a real contrast with the earlier conversions of Kent and the Franks you studied in Module 2, which came through royal example rather than conquest.

By the 790s, Charlemagne ruled the largest territory united under one government in Western Europe since the fall of Rome: modern France, the Low Countries, much of Germany and Italy, and parts of Spain.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Charlemagne is remembered both as a builder of learning and as a brutal conqueror who forced conversion by the sword. How should a historian hold both facts together?
📚 PART 2 · CHRISTMAS DAY, 800

In 799, Pope Leo III, facing a hostile faction in Rome that had assaulted him, fled to Charlemagne for protection. Charlemagne restored him to his position, and on Christmas Day 800, as Charlemagne knelt in prayer at Saint Peter's, Leo placed a crown on his head and proclaimed him Imperator Romanorum: Emperor of the Romans. According to Einhard, Charlemagne's own biographer, whom you will read tomorrow, Charlemagne claimed he would not have entered the church that day had he known the pope's intention.

Was Einhard's claim true, or a piece of careful reputation management after the fact? We cannot know for certain: another example of genuine historical uncertainty you should sit with rather than resolve too quickly. What is certain is the political logic behind the coronation: the Byzantine throne was at that moment held by Empress Irene, and many in the West, rightly or wrongly, used this to argue the imperial throne was effectively vacant, since (in their view) a woman could not rightfully hold it. Leo's coronation of Charlemagne asserted, in effect, that the true seat of Roman imperial authority in the West had returned to a Frankish, Latin-speaking, and papally-sponsored throne: a claim the Byzantines, who never accepted it, considered outright usurpation.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Whatever Charlemagne's private feelings, the coronation of 800 created a lasting Western institution: the idea of a Holy Roman Emperor, crowned by the Pope, as the secular protector of Christendom. You will see this institution and its tensions with the papacy return, sharply, in Week 8.
