Roots of England: The Anglo-Saxon Settlement
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Rome left Britain in 410. What filled the vacuum was not one kingdom but seven, fighting, converting, and slowly becoming something we would recognize as England.

When the legions withdrew from Britain around 410 (the same year Alaric sacked Rome) Romano-British society was left to defend itself against raiders and, soon, settlers: the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, Germanic peoples crossing the North Sea from what is now Denmark and northern Germany. Over the following two centuries they pushed Romano-British and Celtic populations into the western and northern fringes of the island (the ancestors of the Welsh, Cornish, and some Scots), while carving out a patchwork of small Anglo-Saxon kingdoms across the lowlands.

📚 PART 1 · THE HEPTARCHY

By the 7th century, this patchwork had consolidated into roughly seven principal kingdoms (Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Essex, Kent, Sussex, and Wessex) a period historians later nicknamed the Heptarchy (“rule of seven”). No single king ruled all of England; supremacy passed back and forth between rival kingdoms for centuries, decided as often by battle and marriage alliance as by any settled principle of succession. There was no England yet, only English-speaking peoples who did not yet think of themselves as one nation.

What began knitting these rival kingdoms into a shared civilization was not politics but religion. In 597, Pope Gregory the Great sent a monk named Augustine (not the same Augustine you read in Week 1) to Kent to convert the Anglo-Saxons. Kent's king, Aethelberht, whose wife was already a Christian Frankish princess, converted along with his court, and Christianity spread outward from Kent over the following century, reaching every Anglo-Saxon kingdom by around 686.

📚 PART 2 · ONE FAITH, MANY KINGDOMS

Christianization did more than change belief. It gave the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms a shared church calendar, shared Latin literacy through monasteries, and a shared sense (however slow to develop politically) that they belonged to one Christian people even while remaining politically divided. The Venerable Bede, a Northumbrian monk whose Ecclesiastical History of the English People (completed 731) you will read this week, was the first writer to use the term “English” (gens Anglorum) to describe all these peoples together, well before any king ruled them as one kingdom. This is an early, important piece of evidence for standard W.2.16: a national identity can begin to form culturally and religiously long before it exists politically.

EVALUATIVE CHECK
Test your understanding.
What united the seven Anglo-Saxon kingdoms culturally, even while they remained politically divided and often at war?
💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Bede calling scattered, warring kingdoms “the English people” is a reminder that a nation's identity and its political unification are two different processes, and one can run far ahead of the other. Keep this distinction in mind: you will meet it again when the Vikings, and later the Normans, force the political unification Bede's word had already imagined.
