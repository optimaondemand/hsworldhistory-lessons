The Umayyad Caliphate: Conquest, Governance, and the Franks
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
By 750, an Islamic empire stretched from the Atlantic coast of Spain to the Indus River: the largest empire the world had yet seen. It stopped in Western Europe at a single battlefield in France.

After Muhammad's death, a series of caliphs (successors) directed a wave of conquest that, within a single generation, took Syria, Egypt, Persia, and North Africa from Byzantine and Persian rule. In 661, the Umayyad dynasty took control of the caliphate and moved its capital to Damascus, ruling an empire that by 711 had crossed into Spain, toppling the Visigothic kingdom in just a few years, and by 732 had pushed as far as central France.

📚 PART 1 · GOVERNING A VAST EMPIRE

The Umayyads governed their enormous, religiously diverse territory with a mix of firmness and pragmatism. Conquered Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians retained their own courts and clergy under dhimmi status in exchange for the jizya, a tax non-Muslims paid in place of the obligations (like almsgiving and military service) required of Muslims. Conversion brought real legal and social advantages, but it was not typically forced outright, and large Christian and Jewish populations persisted for centuries under Muslim rule, particularly in Egypt and the Levant. Arabic gradually became the administrative and everyday language across much of the conquered territory, a slower and more durable transformation than the conquest itself.

In 732, an Umayyad raiding force pushed north from Spain into the Frankish kingdom of Aquitaine and was met near Tours by the Frankish army under Charles Martel (“the Hammer”), the Mayor of the Palace you met last week. The Franks won decisively. Historians debate how close this battle actually came to changing the future of Western Europe (it was likely a large raid rather than a full invasion attempt) but at the time and ever after, it was remembered in the West as the moment Islamic expansion into Western Europe was checked, and it made Charles Martel's family, the Carolingians, the military saviors of Christendom in the popular imagination.

📚 PART 2 · TOWARD A GOLDEN AGE

In 750, the Umayyad dynasty was overthrown by the Abbasid family, who moved the caliphate's capital east to a newly built city, Baghdad. Under early Abbasid rule, Baghdad became the center of what is often called the Islamic Golden Age: a period, beginning in the mid-8th century and lasting several centuries, of extraordinary achievement in mathematics (the word “algebra” comes from the Arabic al-jabr), medicine, astronomy, and philosophy, much of it produced at Baghdad's House of Wisdom, where scholars translated and built upon Greek, Persian, and Indian learning that had been lost or neglected in the Latin West. Figures like al-Khwarizmi (mathematics), Ibn Sina/Avicenna (medicine and philosophy), and al-Razi (medicine) produced work that would eventually reach and transform European scholarship centuries later, through exactly the kind of Islamic-Western contact zones (Spain, Sicily, and the Crusader states) that this course will keep revisiting.

Notably, one Umayyad prince escaped the Abbasid purge and fled to Spain, founding an independent Umayyad emirate at Cordoba that would itself become a major center of learning and, centuries later, the object of the Christian Reconquista: the slow reconquest of the Iberian peninsula that would help forge a distinct Spanish identity, a story that belongs to the same standard (W.2.16) as England's and France's national development.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Notice the geography here: Byzantium loses territory in the east and south, while the Franks check the advance in the west. Islam's expansion is not a story that happens “elsewhere” from Western civilization: it is one of the central forces shaping the West's own borders, alliances, and eventual encounters, from Charles Martel to the Crusades you will study in Week 9.
