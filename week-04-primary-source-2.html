The Chronicle of 754 (Mozarabic Chronicle)
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Written by an anonymous Christian cleric in Umayyad-ruled Spain, this Latin chronicle is our earliest surviving account of the Battle of Tours, written only about two decades after the battle itself.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Chronicle of 754, on the Battle of Tours, 732 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Kenneth Baxter Wolf, 1990 (public domain-era text; excerpt paraphrased for classroom use).
“And [Abd al-Rahman] and his army crossed the Pyrenees, whereupon Eudo, duke of Aquitaine, having been defeated, fled… Then Charles [Martel] gathered his forces and set out against them… The people of Austrasia, powerful in numbers and formidable in war, having their leader at their head, plunged into battle… And in the shock of the battle the Europeans remained victorious… They killed the king Abd al-Rahman… Suddenly, in the still of the night, the Saracens, seeing the numbers of the dead, feared that if daylight came they would face still greater destruction; so they set up their tents and secretly during the depths of night withdrew, seeking their own land.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Note the chronicler's phrase “the Europeans remained victorious” (Europenses in the Latin): one of the earliest recorded uses of a word for “Europeans” as a collective identity, arguably called into being by exactly this kind of frontier conflict.
