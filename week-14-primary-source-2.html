💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Machiavelli wrote his History of Florence in the 1520s, commissioned by a Medici pope, but he did not shy away from describing the city's recurring internal factionalism with a critical, analytical eye.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
History of Florence, Book III (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. after W. K. Marriott, 1908 (public domain-era translation); excerpt.
“In other republics, the ambitions of powerful citizens usually seek to advance the common good… but in Florence, this was rarely so; the divisions were commonly begun by a few men of great families, seeking to increase their own power at the expense of the public… and it was ever the fate of our city that when one party was overthrown, the victors did not learn moderation from what they themselves had suffered when weak, but instead used their new power with the same excess as their predecessors had used theirs.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Machiavelli here applies the Classical Republican standard of judgment this course uses throughout: he is asking whether Florence's factions served the common good or only their own advantage: and answering, unflinchingly, that they usually did not.
