Henry VIII and the English Reformation
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
England's break with Rome began not with a theological dispute, but with a king's desperate need for a son.

King Henry VIII of England, whose Tudor dynasty had only recently ended decades of civil war (the Wars of the Roses) and desperately needed a secure male heir to avoid renewed instability, sought an annulment of his marriage to Catherine of Aragon, who had not produced a surviving son, so he could marry Anne Boleyn. When Pope Clement VII, under heavy political pressure from Catherine's nephew, the Holy Roman Emperor Charles V, refused to grant the annulment, Henry took a drastic step: rather than simply defying the pope on this one matter, he had Parliament pass the Act of Supremacy in 1534, declaring the king, not the pope, the supreme head of the Church of England.

📚 PART 1 · DISSOLUTION AND DEFIANCE

Henry's break with Rome was, at least initially, more a matter of political and personal authority than deep theological conviction; unlike Luther or Calvin, Henry retained much traditional Catholic doctrine and practice for years afterward. Its material consequences were nonetheless enormous: between 1536 and 1541, Henry ordered the dissolution of the monasteries, seizing the wealth, land, and buildings of England's monastic houses (the very institutions you studied as centers of education, charity, and stability back in Module 3) and redistributing much of that land to loyal nobles, permanently reshaping the English aristocracy's landholding and enriching the crown.

Not everyone accepted the new order quietly. Thomas More, the humanist scholar and former Lord Chancellor whose book Utopia you will read today, refused to sign an oath recognizing Henry's supremacy over the church, and was executed for treason in 1535. According to tradition, More told the executioner his beard had committed no treason and should be spared the axe: a final, characteristically wry defiance from a man who had spent his career defending both his conscience and his sense of humor.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Henry VIII's break with Rome began over a personal and dynastic matter (his need for a male heir), not primarily a theological dispute like Luther's. What does this suggest about the causes of the English Reformation compared to the continental one?
📚 PART 2 · UTOPIA'S CRITIQUE

Written years before his execution, in 1516, More's Utopia describes an imagined ideal island society through a fictional traveler's account, using the device to critique real abuses in English society, particularly the enclosure of common farmland for sheep pasture, which was displacing poor farmers to profit wealthy landowners. This satirical, humanist method (using an invented society to hold up a critical mirror to one's own) reflects exactly the confident, reason-driven, classically-informed intellectual style you studied in the Renaissance humanism of Module 7, now turned toward social and political criticism.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

More's fate (a brilliant, principled humanist executed by the king he had once served loyally) is the first half of a pattern this course asks you to weigh fully tomorrow, when you meet the second Thomas whose story runs alongside his.
