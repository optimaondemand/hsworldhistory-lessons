Francis Bacon, New Atlantis
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Bacon, an English philosopher and statesman, published this unfinished utopian fiction in 1627, describing an imagined research institution, “Salomon's House,” dedicated to organized, cooperative scientific investigation of nature: a vision that would help inspire the actual founding of Britain's Royal Society decades later.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
New Atlantis (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Original 1627 English text (public domain). Excerpt.
“The End of our Foundation is the knowledge of causes, and secret motions of things; and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire, to the effecting of all things possible… We have also, and do procure, of certain instruments, which the vulgar call engines of experiments… We do also declare… those things which we do not publish, are such as are new indeed in themselves, and yet may be excused from prohibition… and this we do also, that hereby it may not be conceived, that we know only that which is vulgarly known.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Bacon's phrase “the knowledge of causes” and his emphasis on shared “instruments” and organized investigation describe an inductive, evidence-first method very different from scholastic argument from established authority: the method behind Kepler's and, as you will read tomorrow, Galileo's own work.
