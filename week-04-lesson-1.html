Muhammad and the Rise of Islam
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In little more than a century, a religious movement born among the merchants and tribes of Arabia would rule territory from Spain to the borders of India.

Muhammad ibn Abdullah was born around 570 in Mecca, an important caravan and pilgrimage city in the Arabian peninsula, home to a shrine, the Kaaba, that already drew worshippers of many tribal gods. Around 610, Muhammad began to receive what he and his followers understood as revelations from God (Allah, the same Arabic word Arabic-speaking Christians and Jews use for God), later collected as the Qur'an. His message was uncompromisingly monotheistic in a polytheistic society, and it drew both converts and fierce opposition from Mecca's ruling clans.

📚 PART 1 · THE HIJRA AND THE UMMA

In 622, facing persecution, Muhammad and his followers migrated from Mecca to the city of Yathrib, later renamed Medina. This migration, the Hijra, marks year one of the Islamic calendar: not Muhammad's birth, and not the first revelation, but the founding of a self-governing religious and political community, the umma. This is a telling choice: Islam, from its earliest years, understood itself as a faith meant to organize a political community, not merely a private belief. By the time of Muhammad's death in 632, most of Arabia had converted or submitted to Muslim rule, uniting a peninsula of previously independent, often warring tribes under one faith and one leadership for the first time in its history.

Core Muslim practice crystallized around what later tradition calls the Five Pillars: the declaration of faith (shahada), five daily prayers, almsgiving, fasting during the month of Ramadan, and pilgrimage to Mecca (hajj) at least once if able.

📚 PART 2 · ONE GOD, THREE FAITHS

Islam did not emerge in a religious vacuum. It shares with Judaism and Christianity a common commitment to strict monotheism, a shared reverence for many of the same figures: Abraham, Moses, and Jesus (whom Islam honors as a major prophet, though not as divine), and a common belief in a final judgment. The Qur'an explicitly identifies Jews and Christians as “People of the Book,” recipients of genuine earlier revelation, and prescribes them a distinct, though subordinate, legal status (dhimmi) under Muslim rule rather than forced conversion.

The real disagreements are just as important to understand accurately as the agreements. Christianity holds Jesus to be the divine Son of God, crucified and resurrected; Islam denies his divinity and his crucifixion, honoring him instead as a prophet who foretold Muhammad. Judaism and Islam share a stricter, non-Trinitarian monotheism and dietary and legal traditions with real structural similarities, but differ on which prophet and revelation is final. Comparing these traditions honestly means neither flattening real disagreements into “we all really believe the same thing” nor treating the traditions as having nothing whatsoever in common.
