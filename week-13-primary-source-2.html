💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Plato's Republic, written in Athens in the 4th century BC, was exactly the kind of text Plethon's Florence lectures helped bring to renewed Western attention. This excerpt is from the famous Allegory of the Cave, in Book VII.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Republic, Book VII, the Allegory of the Cave (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1871 (public domain). Excerpt.
“Behold! human beings living in an underground den… here they have been from their childhood, having their necks and legs chained… and can only see before them, being prevented by the chains from turning round their heads… And suppose further that the prison has an echo which returns from the wall… to them, I said, the truth would be literally nothing but the shadows of the images… And now look again, and see what naturally follows if the prisoners are released and disabused of their error. At first, when any of them is liberated… and forced to look toward the light, he will suffer sharp pains; the glare will distress him.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Plethon's Florentine audience, steeped for centuries in mostly secondhand knowledge of Plato through Latin summaries and Arabic commentary, was encountering arguments like this one directly, in a form far closer to the original, for the first time in a very long while.
