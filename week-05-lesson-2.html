The Carolingian Renaissance

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Charlemagne could not read fluently and never learned to write well. He nonetheless launched the most significant revival of learning Western Europe had seen since Rome's fall.

By the late 8th century, literacy and classical learning had decayed badly across the former Western Empire; even many clergy struggled with correct Latin. Charlemagne, aware of this decline and determined to have an educated clergy capable of properly administering sacraments and interpreting Scripture, gathered scholars from across Europe to his court at Aachen, most famously Alcuin of York, an English scholar-monk who became the intellectual director of what historians now call the Carolingian Renaissance.

📚 PART 1 · A DELIBERATE REVIVAL

In 789, Charlemagne issued the Admonitio Generalis (“General Admonition”), a sweeping capitulary (royal decree) that, among other reforms, ordered every monastery and cathedral to maintain a school for training clergy and instructed monks to correct and standardize the badly corrupted texts of Scripture and the church fathers that had accumulated through centuries of careless copying. This was not learning for its own sake in some abstract sense; it was learning in direct service of good government and right worship: further evidence, in the spirit of this course's approach to history, that ideas and institutions reinforce each other rather than one simply causing the other.

The practical results were remarkable. Carolingian scribes developed a new, cleaner, more legible handwriting style called Carolingian minuscule, with clearly separated lowercase letters: the direct ancestor of the lowercase letters this lesson is printed in today. Monastic scriptoria across the empire began systematically copying classical Latin texts, and a very large share of the ancient Roman literature that survives today exists only because Carolingian monks copied it in the 8th and 9th centuries. Without this deliberate act of preservation, much of what we know of Roman literature would simply be lost.

📚 PART 2 · SYNTHESIS, NOT SUBSTITUTION

The Carolingian Renaissance is a clear, compact example of the larger thesis this course has been building since Week 1: Western civilization arose from a genuine synthesis of Greco-Roman learning, Judeo-Christian faith, and the political and social structures of Germanic peoples like the Franks. Alcuin's classroom curriculum, the trivium and quadrivium (grammar, rhetoric, logic, arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy), was inherited directly from Roman education. The purpose of that curriculum was overwhelmingly religious, training clergy and administrators for a Christian kingdom. And the political structure funding and enforcing it all (royal capitularies, a warrior aristocracy granted land in exchange for service) was distinctly Frankish and Germanic, not Roman in origin.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

None of these three elements alone produced the Carolingian Renaissance. It took all three together. Keep this synthesis in mind: it is the same formula, differently mixed, you will see recur across medieval and early modern Western Europe for the rest of this course.
