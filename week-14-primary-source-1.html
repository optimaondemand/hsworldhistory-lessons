Week 14 · Primary Source 1
Leon Battista Alberti, On the Art of Building
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Alberti, a Florentine humanist scholar and architect writing in the 1450s, drew directly on the ancient Roman architect Vitruvius to argue that beauty in building followed discoverable mathematical rules, not mere custom or personal taste: a claim reflecting the era's growing confidence in human reason.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
On the Art of Building, Book VI, Chapter 2 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Joseph Rykwert, Neil Leach, and Robert Tavernor, 1988 (scholarly translation of a 15th-century Latin text); excerpt.
“Beauty is a form of sympathy and consonance of the parts within a body, according to definite number, outline, and position, as dictated by concinnitas [harmony], the absolute and fundamental rule in Nature… This is the main object of the art of building, and the source of her dignity, charm, authority, and worth.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice Alberti's confidence that beauty follows “definite number” and rule, discoverable through reason, exactly like the mathematical proofs you might associate with Ptolemy or Euclid. This confidence in ordered, rational human achievement is a hallmark of Renaissance humanism.
