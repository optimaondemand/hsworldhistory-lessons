Augustine of Hippo, City of God
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Rome's sack in 410 raised an urgent question for contemporaries: if Rome had abandoned her old gods for Christ, why had Christ let her fall? Pagan critics blamed the new religion. Augustine, bishop of Hippo in North Africa, spent over a decade answering them in his massive work The City of God. This excerpt is from the opening argument.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
City of God, Book I, Chapters 1 and 7 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Marcus Dods, 1871 (public domain). Excerpt.
“The glorious city of God is my theme in this work... I have undertaken its defense against those who prefer their own gods to the Founder of this city… For it is a matter of history, that when Rome was sacked, this savagery was proved to have arisen from the customs of war, while whatever was gentle, and by the custom of war beyond expectation, must be credited to the name of Christ. In the sack of the city, wherever the law of war allowed, cruelties were being perpetrated... but wherever it was Christ's name that stayed the hand, there the barbarians showed a violence which put civilized conquerors to shame. For it was in Christ's name, and by the influence of Christian times, that the enemy showed such mercy as to select the largest and most frequented churches, and set them apart to be sanctuaries of pity, into which no enemy should burst, from which none should be dragged who had fled thither, into which many, that were not Christians, might be gathered by their friends, so as to escape a violent death, and from which no one who dared to drag out a captive should be prevented from meeting his own death instead.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice what Augustine is doing here: he is not denying that Rome was sacked, or that Christians suffered. He is reframing the meaning of the event, and he is using a specific fact you already know from Monday's lesson (Alaric's order to spare those who took refuge in the churches) as evidence for his larger argument.
