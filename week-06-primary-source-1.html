Week 6 · Primary Source 1
The Rule of St. Benedict
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Written around 529 for Benedict's own monastery at Monte Cassino, this Rule became the standard guide for Western monastic life by the 9th century. Notice its practical, moderate tone: a real contrast to more extreme forms of ascetic self-denial practiced elsewhere in the early Christian world.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Rule of St. Benedict, Chapters 48 and 53 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Leonard J. Doyle, 1948 (translation of a public-domain-era Latin text); excerpt.
“Idleness is the enemy of the soul. Therefore the brothers should be occupied at certain times in manual labor, and at other times in sacred reading… And if the circumstances of the place or their poverty should require that they themselves do the work of gathering the harvest, they should not be discontented; for then are they truly monks when they live by the labor of their hands, as did our fathers and the apostles.”

“All guests who present themselves are to be welcomed as Christ Himself, for He is going to say, ‘I came as a guest, and you received Me’… Great care and concern are to be shown in receiving poor people and pilgrims, because in them more particularly Christ is received.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice how the Rule treats manual labor as spiritually valuable, not beneath the monks' dignity, and mandates hospitality to travelers and the poor as a religious duty. Both instructions had large, practical social consequences well beyond the monastery walls.
