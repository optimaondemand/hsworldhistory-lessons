1066: Conquest and the Making of Angevin England
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
One year, three claimants, two great battles, and a kingdom permanently transformed. 1066 is the most consequential single year in English history.

When the childless King Edward the Confessor died in January 1066, at least three men believed the English throne was rightfully theirs: Harold Godwinson, England's most powerful earl, crowned almost immediately by the English council; Harald Hardrada, king of Norway, claiming an old inheritance agreement; and William, Duke of Normandy, who claimed Edward had personally promised him the throne years earlier and that Harold had sworn an oath to support that claim.

📚 PART 1 · TWO BATTLES IN THREE WEEKS

Harold moved first against Hardrada's Norwegian invasion in the north, winning a crushing victory at the Battle of Stamford Bridge in late September. Almost immediately, word arrived that William had landed in the south. Harold marched his exhausted army over 240 miles in roughly two weeks and met William near Hastings on 14 October 1066. After a long day of fighting, Harold was killed (according to legend, though not certainly, shot through the eye with an arrow) and his army broke. William was crowned king on Christmas Day, 1066, echoing, whether deliberately or not, Charlemagne's own coronation on the same feast day 266 years earlier.

The Norman Conquest transformed English society from the top down. William replaced almost the entire Anglo-Saxon landholding aristocracy with Norman nobles, imposed a rigorous feudal land system across the kingdom, and in 1086 ordered the compilation of the Domesday Book, an extraordinarily detailed survey of nearly every landholding, resource, and taxable asset in England: a demonstration of administrative ambition and central control unmatched anywhere else in contemporary Western Europe.

📚 PART 2 · TOWARD THE ANGEVIN EMPIRE AND THE RULE OF LAW

The Norman line eventually gave way, through marriage and inheritance, to the Angevin dynasty under Henry II (reigned 1154–1189), who ruled not only England but a vast collection of French territories, an assemblage sometimes called the Angevin Empire. Henry II's most lasting achievement was legal: he expanded royal courts and standardized legal procedure across England, creating the beginnings of what is still called common law: law built up from accumulated royal court decisions and applied uniformly across the kingdom, rather than a patchwork of purely local custom. This built directly on Alfred's earlier law-making and represents a real advance in the Classical Republican criterion of “administering common laws that regularly and predictably resolve conflicts of interest between citizens.”

Henry's expansion of royal justice did not go unchallenged. His court included scholars like John of Salisbury, whose treatise on politics, the Policraticus, which you will read today, directly addressed the central question this era kept raising: what happens when a king's power grows too great, and what distinguishes a lawful king from a tyrant? Henry's own reign would soon provide a dramatic test case, when his conflict with his former friend and Archbishop of Canterbury, Thomas Becket, ended in Becket's murder in his own cathedral in 1170: a scandal that forced Henry into public penance and set real limits on how far a king could push the church.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

England by 1170 combined French-speaking Norman nobility, Anglo-Saxon legal inheritance, and an increasingly assertive, systematized royal court: the specific institutional mixture, W.2.18 asks you to notice, that eventually grows into England's distinctive constitutional and legal tradition.
