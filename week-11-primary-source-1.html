The 1200 Charter of Ipswich
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Granted by King John in 1200, this charter is one of the earliest surviving English borough charters establishing detailed self-government, including the town's right to elect its own officials (portmen and bailiffs) and hold its own court.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Charter of Ipswich, 1200 (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. after C. Gross, The Gild Merchant, 1890 (public-domain-era scholarly translation); excerpt.
“Know that we have granted to our free burgesses of Ipswich, that they may have a Guild Merchant, with all liberties and free customs belonging to such a guild… and that they may choose from among themselves two of the more discreet men to be bailiffs of the said town, and four of the more discreet and lawful men to keep pleas of our crown, and to be justices with the bailiffs of that town… and that they shall not be impleaded outside the walls of the said town concerning any tenement within the said town…”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice that the townspeople elect their own officials and cannot be dragged into a court outside their own town over local property matters. This is real, meaningful self-government, granted by royal authority rather than a feudal lord's.
