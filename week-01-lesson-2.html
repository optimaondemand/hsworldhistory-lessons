Vandals, Attila, and the End in the West
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Rome's western government did not die in a single dramatic battle. It faded, region by region, until one day there was simply no emperor left to depose.

Alaric's sack in 410 was a wound, not a killing blow. The West staggered on for another 66 years, but the pattern of 410 repeated and worsened. In 429, a Vandal king named Gaiseric led his people across the Strait of Gibraltar into Roman North Africa (the empire's richest grain-producing province) and by 439 had carved out an independent Vandal kingdom there, cutting off Rome's food supply. In 455, Gaiseric's fleet sailed on Rome itself. Unlike Alaric, Gaiseric made no promise to spare the churches; his sack was longer and harsher, and it is from his name that we get the word vandalism.

📚 PART 1 · THE SCOURGE OF GOD

At the same time, an even greater threat loomed from the Hunnic king Attila, who had unified the Hunnic tribes into the most feared military power in Europe. In 451, Attila invaded Gaul, where a Roman general named Aetius (sometimes called “the last of the Romans”) assembled an unlikely coalition of Romans and their former enemies, the Visigoths, and stopped Attila at the Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (also called Chalons). It was one of the last real Roman military victories in the West, and it required allying with the very peoples Rome had spent decades fighting.

The following year, Attila invaded Italy instead. Tradition holds that Pope Leo I met Attila in person and persuaded him to turn back, though plague and supply shortages among the Huns were likely just as decisive. Attila died in 453, and his empire, which existed to serve his personal authority and had no institutions to outlast him, collapsed within a few years. This is a clean example of the “Great Men” principle at work: Attila's empire was not a durable state built on law or shared culture. It was one man's personal power, and it evaporated the moment he did.

📚 PART 2 · A WHIMPER, NOT A BANG

The formal end, when it came in 476, was almost anticlimactic. A Germanic officer in Roman service named Odoacer deposed the boy-emperor Romulus Augustulus (whose name, by coincidence, combined the names of Rome's legendary founder and its first emperor) and did not bother naming a new emperor at all. He sent the imperial regalia to Constantinople, telling the Eastern emperor that one emperor was enough for the whole Roman world. Many people alive at the time may not have registered 476 as a turning point; local Roman institutions, landowners, and even the Senate persisted for a while under new Germanic kings who often kept Roman law and administration running.

But the substance of change was real, even without a dramatic final battle. Central taxation, a professional army answering to one government, long-distance trade, and widespread literacy all declined sharply across the former Western provinces over the following century. This was not civilization being replaced by a single “dark age” everywhere at once (some regions, like Ostrogothic Italy under Theodoric, initially preserved a great deal of Roman culture) but the shared political structure that had bound Western Europe together for five centuries was gone, and it would not be fully rebuilt. As you will see this quarter, the peoples who divided the West (Franks, Visigoths, Anglo-Saxons, and others) each began forming the kingdoms that would eventually become recognizably France, Spain, and England.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Notice the shape of this story: no single cause, no single date, and no single villain. Gaiseric, Attila, Aetius, and Odoacer each made choices that mattered, and each could have chosen otherwise. The collapse of Western Rome changed Europe permanently: but it was not a project anyone set out to accomplish, and it was not the working-out of some grand law of history. It was contingency, compounding over seven decades.
