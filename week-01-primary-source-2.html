Jordanes, The Origin and Deeds of the Goths (Getica)
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Jordanes was a Roman official of Gothic descent writing around 551, more than a century after the events of this week's lesson, working from an earlier (lost) history by Cassiodorus. His account blends real history with legend about Gothic origins, but his description of Attila's death and its aftermath is one of the fullest surviving accounts.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Getica, on the death of Attila (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Charles C. Mierow, 1915 (public domain). Excerpt.
“Attila, king of the Huns and ravager of the provinces of Europe, that terror of the world… was found dead by the effusion of blood, without any wound, and the girl with downcast face weeping beneath her veil… When he was mourned with such lamentations, a ‘strava,’ as they call it, was celebrated over his remains with great revelling. They gave way in turn to the extremes of feeling and displayed funereal grief alternating with joy. Then in the secrecy of night they buried his body in the earth. They bound his coffins, one with gold, a second with silver, and a third with the strength of iron… and swift death was the reward of their labor, for they who buried him were slain.”

“Now this Attila was a man born into the world to shake the nations, the scourge of all lands, who in some way terrified all mankind by the dreadful rumors noised abroad concerning him… Yet in his character there was a mixture of craft and simplicity, and it is not surprising that so great and warlike a nation should have been won by him alone.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Notice the detail that Attila's burial party was killed to keep his grave's location secret: whether literally true or embellished, it tells you something about how contemporaries understood the fragility of power built on one man's fearsome reputation rather than lasting institutions.
