The Viking Age: Raiders, Traders, and Alfred's Stand
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In 793, raiders from the sea struck a monastery so suddenly and violently that a scholar abroad wrote: “Never before has such terror appeared.” England had just met the Vikings.

The Viking raid on the monastery of Lindisfarne in 793 opened nearly three centuries of Scandinavian raiding, trading, and settlement across the British Isles, Frankish territory, and beyond, reaching as far as Russia, the Mediterranean, and North America. Vikings were not merely raiders; the same seafaring skill that let them strike undefended coastal monasteries also built extensive trade networks and, eventually, permanent settlements: including, as you will recall from Week 4's Frankish frontier, the Viking leader Rollo's settlement that became Normandy in 911.

📚 PART 1 · THE GREAT HEATHEN ARMY

By the 860s, Viking raiding in England had escalated into full conquest. A large, coordinated Danish force nicknamed by later chroniclers the “Great Heathen Army” overran three of the four major Anglo-Saxon kingdoms (Northumbria, East Anglia, and Mercia) leaving only Wessex, in the southwest, still independent under its young king, Alfred. In 878, Alfred was nearly driven from his own kingdom entirely, reduced to hiding in the marshes of Somerset. From this low point, he regrouped, raised an army, and won a decisive victory over the Danish leader Guthrum at the Battle of Edington.

Alfred's response to victory was as important as the victory itself. Rather than attempting to exterminate or fully expel the Danes, he negotiated a treaty establishing the Danelaw, a boundary line east of which Danish law and settlement would be tolerated, in exchange for Guthrum's baptism and a lasting peace. He then built a network of fortified towns (burhs) across Wessex, reorganized his military into a standing, rotating defense force, and, remarkably for a warrior-king, personally sponsored the translation of Latin texts into English and promoted literacy and learning at his court: a modest, wartime echo of Charlemagne's own cultural program a century earlier.

QUICK CHECK-IN
Alfred chose to negotiate a boundary with the Danes rather than fight to the last man for total victory. What does this choice suggest about his priorities as a ruler?
📚 PART 2 · ALFRED THE GREAT

Alfred is the only English monarch ever given the epithet “the Great,” and the reasons connect directly to the Classical Republican standard this course applies throughout: he defended his kingdom against an existential external threat, he built durable institutions (fortified towns, a reformed military, a law code drawing on earlier Anglo-Saxon, Mosaic, and Christian precedent) that outlasted his own reign, and he cultivated a shared cultural and legal identity among the English that helped his successors, over the following century, gradually reconquer the Danelaw and unite all of England under one crown for the first time. This is the direct fulfillment of Bede's word, “the English people,” which you met in Week 3 as a cultural claim well ahead of any political reality: a reality Alfred and his heirs would begin to build.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

England's unification did not arrive as a single conquest, but as a two-century process kicked off by Alfred's stubborn survival at his kingdom's lowest point. Keep his legal and institutional legacy in mind tomorrow, when a very different kind of conquest (sudden, decisive, and foreign) reshapes England again in a single year.
