Ptolemy, The Almagest
Primary Source Reading
💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Written by the Greco-Roman astronomer Claudius Ptolemy around 150 AD in Alexandria, the Almagest set out a detailed mathematical model of a geocentric (Earth-centered) universe that remained the standard astronomical authority in both the Islamic world and, once translated, medieval and Renaissance Europe for roughly 1,400 years, until the Scientific Revolution you will study later this course.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Almagest, Book I (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. G. J. Toomer, 1984 (scholarly translation of a Greek text of ancient origin); excerpt.
“That the heavens move spherically… and that the earth too, taken as a whole, is sensibly spherical… is immediately obvious from the fact that the sun, moon and other stars do not rise and set simultaneously for everyone on earth, but do so earlier for those more towards the east… We shall also find that the earth has the ratio of a point to the distance of the sphere of the so-called fixed stars, since it has no perceptible size in relation to that distance.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Ptolemy correctly reasoned the Earth was spherical and vastly smaller than the distance to the stars, centuries before the printing press or the telescope, purely from careful observation and geometry. Notice this text traveled from Alexandria, through Byzantine and Islamic scholarship, and would soon travel to Italy alongside the Greek scholars you will read about tomorrow.
