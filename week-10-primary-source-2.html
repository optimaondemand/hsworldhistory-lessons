💡 BEFORE YOU READ

The Venetian merchant Marco Polo traveled the Silk Road to Kublai Khan's court in the 1270s, remaining in Mongol-ruled China for roughly seventeen years. His account, dictated to a writer while Polo was later a prisoner of war in Genoa, describes wonders that many contemporary Europeans initially found difficult to believe.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
The Travels of Marco Polo, on the paper currency of the Great Khan (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. Henry Yule, 1871 (public domain). Excerpt.
“In this city of Kanbalu [Beijing] is the mint of the Great Khan, who may truly be said to possess the secret of the alchemists… He causes the bark to be stripped from mulberry trees… made into a substance like paper, but black… and this he causes to be cut into pieces of money… And all these pieces of paper are issued with as much solemnity and authority as if they were of pure gold or silver… and indeed everybody takes them readily, for wheresoever a person may go throughout the Great Khan's dominions he shall find these pieces of paper current, and shall be able to transact all sales and purchases of goods by means of them just as well as if they were coins of pure gold.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Paper currency, backed entirely by imperial authority rather than precious metal, was a genuine Chinese innovation centuries ahead of its adoption in Europe. Notice how carefully Marco Polo explains a system his European readers would have found almost unbelievable.
