Turning Points: A World Remade, 1206–1648
Tier 3 · VR component + written component · due at the end of Quarter 2 / the Fall Semester
🎯 PROJECT OVERVIEW

Across Quarter 2 you built six Turning Points exhibits: the Mongol Storm, the Black Death, Constantinople Encircled, Florence, Wittenberg, and the Two Thomases. This week's lessons add a seventh, unbuilt turning point (the Thirty Years War and the Peace of Westphalia) for you to add yourself. This final capstone asks you to assemble all seven into one gallery and to answer, using the whole Fall Semester's evidence from Week 1 to Week 18, this course's central historiographical question.

🎯 ESSENTIAL QUESTION
Across nearly twelve centuries, from the fall of Rome to the Peace of Westphalia, what actually drives historical change: the deliberate designs of great figures, the slow pressure of ideas and material conditions, or contingency: particular people making particular choices that could easily have gone otherwise?

📝 WRITTEN COMPONENT (M365 Word Document)

Write a 700–900 word essay answering the essential question directly, citing specific evidence from at least five Turning Points and at least one example from Quarter 1. Your essay should explicitly weigh the three historiographical frameworks this course has used all semester (Great Men and Great Events, the history of ideas and material conditions, and contingency) and should reach a defended conclusion about which explains the Fall Semester's history best, or how they work together.

👨‍🏫 FACILITATOR NOTE

This is the largest single assessment of the Fall Semester; budget real SpeedGrader time and consider spreading grading across several days. Students often default to naming only one historiographical framework as “correct”; the strongest essays will show these frameworks working together (for example, Richelieu’s raison d’état as both a contingent personal choice and evidence of material/political pressure reshaping the state). If a live section is available, a facilitator-led gallery walk comparing several students’ final verdicts makes an excellent closing activity for the semester.
