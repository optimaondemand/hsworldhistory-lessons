Greek Manuscripts and the Council of Florence

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Even as Byzantium was dying, its scholars were carrying its greatest treasure west: the books themselves.

In 1439, more than a decade before Constantinople's final fall, Byzantine Emperor John VIII Palaiologos traveled to Italy for the Council of Florence, a last, desperate attempt to reunite the Orthodox and Catholic churches in exchange for Western military support. The theological reunion the council proclaimed proved short-lived and deeply unpopular back in Constantinople, but the council had an unintended and far more lasting consequence: it brought a delegation of Byzantine scholars, including the philosopher George Gemistos Plethon, into direct, sustained contact with Italian humanists.

📚 PART 1 · PLETHON AND THE REBIRTH OF PLATO

Plethon's lectures on Plato in Florence made a powerful impression on his Italian hosts, particularly Cosimo de' Medici, the city's leading banker and political patron, who was reportedly so inspired that he later funded the establishment of a Platonic Academy in Florence dedicated to translating and studying Plato's works, most of which had been only patchily known in the Latin West for centuries. This week's second primary source, an excerpt from Plato's Republic, represents exactly this kind of text: known to the Byzantine world in the original Greek continuously since antiquity, but only fully recovered by the Latin West through contacts like these in the 15th century.

As Constantinople's position grew more desperate through the 1440s and finally fell in 1453, an accelerating stream of Greek scholars, manuscripts, and refugees fled westward to Italy, carrying not only Plato but Homer, the Greek tragedians, and much else that had survived in the Byzantine world in more complete and reliable forms than what had circulated in the West for centuries. Standard W.4.2 asks you to name this Byzantine contribution specifically, alongside the Classical and Islamic influences you have already studied, as one of the real streams feeding what would become the Italian Renaissance.

EVALUATIVE CHECK
Test your understanding.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Byzantium's fall was also, in a real sense, a transmission: the empire that had preserved Greek learning for a thousand years handed a substantial share of it directly to the civilization that would build the Renaissance, the subject of next week's module.
