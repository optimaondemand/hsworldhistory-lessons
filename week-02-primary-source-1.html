💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Procopius served as legal secretary to Justinian's general Belisarius and traveled with him on campaign. This excerpt describes the Nika Riots of 532, a week-long uprising in Constantinople that nearly overthrew Justinian and burned much of the city, including the original church that Justinian would rebuild as the Hagia Sophia.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
History of the Wars, Book I, on the Nika Riots (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Trans. H. B. Dewing, 1914 (public domain). Excerpt.
“At this time the officers of the city administration in Byzantium were putting to death some of the rioters, and the citizens conceived the desire to rescue those about to be executed… the two factions, conspiring together, and adopting the watchword ‘Nika,’ became suddenly hostile to the government… And the emperor and his court were deliberating as to whether it would be safe for them to remain, or best to flee by boat. And the Empress Theodora also spoke as follows: ‘As to the belief that a woman ought not to be daring among men or to assert herself boldly among those who shrink from danger, I consider that the present crisis most of all does not permit us to discuss whether the matter should be regarded thus or otherwise… May I never be separated from this purple, and may I not live that day on which those who meet me shall not address me as mistress. If now it is your wish to save yourself, O Emperor, there is no difficulty. For we have much money, and there is the sea, here the boats. However consider whether it will not come about after you have been saved that you would gladly exchange that safety for death. For as for myself, I approve a certain ancient saying that royalty is a good burial-shroud.’ When the queen had spoken thus, all were filled with boldness…”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Theodora, a former actress of low social standing whom Justinian had married against custom and law, is the one who steadies the emperor's resolve at the empire's most dangerous hour. Watch how the lesson tomorrow uses this episode to evaluate Justinian's regime by the standard of Classical Republican judgment: could it hold internal order under real pressure?
