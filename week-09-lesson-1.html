The Call to Crusade and the Triumphs at Antioch and Jerusalem
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A pope's sermon in a French field launched an army that marched three thousand miles to capture a city almost none of them had ever seen.

In 1095, Pope Urban II preached a sermon at Clermont, France that would change history. He called on knights and nobles to take up arms and travel to the East. This was no ordinary war. It was a crusade. Urban II called for a holy war to recover lands seen as Christ's own patrimony. A Christian knight could count the fighting itself as an act of penance. Those who took the cross would receive a full indulgence and the same spiritual rewards as pilgrims to Jerusalem.

📚 PART 1 · WHY THEY WENT

Why did so many lords and knights answer the call? First, there was genuine concern about the plight of Christians in the East. Prior to the rise of Islam, Egypt, the Levant, and Anatolia had been Christian homelands, with churches dating to the 1st and 2nd century, all once part of the Roman Empire. After Muslim conquerors took these lands, Christians faced pressure to convert, the jizya tax, and dhimmi status. Recently, Turkish forces had defeated the Byzantine army at Manzikert in 1071, and Christian pilgrims reported harassment at holy sites. When Byzantine Emperor Alexios I appealed to the West for military help, Pope Urban answered with a call for crusade: notable given that only decades earlier, the Great Schism of 1054 had split the Latin and Greek churches (as you may recall touching on in Week 2).

The historian Jonathan Riley-Smith has argued against reading the Crusades as simple plunder expeditions: the evidence shows most crusaders lost wealth on the journey, sold or mortgaged land to finance it, and faced roughly a one-in-three chance of dying. This was an act of self-sacrifice conforming to the knightly ideal of bravery and service, offering spiritual redemption: though material incentives like new feudal lands were never entirely absent either. As always, this course asks you to hold both motives together rather than choosing one.

EVALUATIVE CHECK
Test your understanding of Part 1.
According to Riley-Smith's evidence discussed in this lesson, what does the finding that most crusaders lost wealth suggest about their primary motivation?
📚 PART 2 · ANTIOCH AND JERUSALEM

The First Crusade (1096–1099) was led by great nobles including Godfrey of Bouillon, Bohemond of Taranto, and Raymond of Saint-Gilles. In October 1097 they reached Antioch, enduring a grueling siege before finally storming the city in June 1098: only to be trapped inside by a huge relief army under Kerbogha of Mosul. At this desperate moment, a priest named Peter Bartholomew claimed to have discovered the Holy Lance that had pierced Christ on the cross. Morale soared, and the crusaders marched out and won a stunning victory.

The remaining crusaders reached Jerusalem, then held by the Fatimids of Egypt, in June 1099. On 15 July 1099, after days of prayer and a barefoot procession around the walls, they launched a final assault and took the city. Churches rang bells across Europe in celebration. Godfrey of Bouillon was chosen to rule, taking the modest title “Defender of the Holy Sepulchre” rather than king. The fall of Jerusalem involved a massacre of many inhabitants, as was tragically common when cities fell by storm after long resistance: a fact this course asks you to face honestly rather than excuse, even while trying to understand the brutal norms of siege warfare on all sides in this era.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

A relatively small Western force marched deep into unfamiliar lands and captured cities defended by far larger powers. Muslim disunity, Byzantine logistical support, crusader resilience, and religious conviction all played a part: but the Kingdom of Jerusalem and the Crusader States that emerged were fragile frontier societies from the start, as tomorrow's lesson will show.
