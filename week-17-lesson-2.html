Galileo and the New Physics

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A telescope pointed at Jupiter, and two philosophers asking how anyone can be certain of anything at all, together completed the intellectual revolution Copernicus had begun.

The Italian scientist Galileo Galilei did not invent the telescope, but he was among the first to turn a substantially improved version toward the sky, in 1609–1610, and what he saw directly challenged the old cosmology: mountains and craters on a supposedly “perfect” Moon, spots moving across the face of the Sun, and, most damaging to strict geocentrism, four moons visibly orbiting Jupiter, proving that not everything in the heavens circled the Earth. Galileo also observed the phases of Venus, which matched Copernican predictions far better than Ptolemaic ones.

📚 PART 1 · A COLLISION WITH AUTHORITY

Galileo's outspoken and increasingly public defense of Copernican heliocentrism brought him into direct conflict with church authorities, who by this point treated a literal, geocentric reading of certain biblical passages as settled doctrine. In 1633, the Roman Inquisition tried Galileo, forced him to recant his support for heliocentrism under threat of severe punishment, and placed him under house arrest for the remainder of his life. According to a persistent, probably apocryphal legend, Galileo muttered under his breath after his forced recantation, “Eppur si muove”: “and yet it moves.” Whether or not he actually said it, the phrase captures a genuine and lasting tension this course has traced since the Investiture Controversy in Module 4: what happens when careful empirical observation and institutional religious authority reach different conclusions?

QUICK CHECK-IN
Galileo's telescope observations (Jupiter's moons, Venus's phases) provided direct evidence against strict geocentrism. Why might this kind of evidence have felt more threatening to some church authorities than Copernicus's earlier mathematical model alone?
📚 PART 2 · BACON AND DESCARTES: TWO PATHS TO CERTAINTY

Alongside these empirical discoveries, philosophers worked to explain systematically why the new science's methods actually produced reliable knowledge. Francis Bacon, whom you read yesterday, championed induction: building general conclusions carefully upward from repeated, controlled observation and experiment. The French philosopher René Descartes took a different, more purely rational path in his 1641 Meditations, which you will read today: doubting everything he possibly could, including the reliability of his own senses, until he found one thing he judged impossible to doubt: the very fact that he was doubting, and therefore thinking, at all. His famous conclusion, cogito, ergo sum (“I think, therefore I am”), aimed to rebuild certain knowledge from an unshakeable rational foundation, rather than from sensory observation alone.

Bacon's empiricism and Descartes's rationalism represent two distinct, sometimes rival, accounts of how humans can reliably know the natural world: a foundational disagreement in what we would now call the philosophy of science that has never been fully resolved, and that still underlies real debates in scientific and philosophical method today.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Galileo's trial and Bacon's and Descartes's competing methods together mark the definitive arrival of a new intellectual world: one in which observation, mathematics, and systematic doubt, rather than inherited authority alone, increasingly determined what educated people believed about nature. This confidence in human reason, which you have now traced from the Carolingian Renaissance through Renaissance humanism to this new science, would soon collide with a very different force: the religious and political violence of the war you will study next week.
