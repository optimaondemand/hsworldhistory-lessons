Justinian and Theodora: Reconquest, Law, and Plague

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
One emperor tried to restore the entire Roman world by the sword and by the law book at once. He nearly succeeded at both, and the cost of trying nearly broke his empire.

Justinian I, who ruled from 527 to 565, inherited an empire that had just survived the Nika riots you read about yesterday. He used that survival as a mandate for the most ambitious program of any Byzantine ruler: reconquering the western provinces lost to Vandals and Ostrogoths, and codifying Roman law once and for all.

📚 PART 1 · RECONQUEST AND LAW

Justinian's general Belisarius (whom you met briefly in yesterday's source) won stunning early victories, destroying the Vandal kingdom in North Africa in 534 and beginning the reconquest of Italy from the Ostrogoths soon after. For a moment, it seemed the Roman Mediterranean might truly be restored. But Italy's reconquest dragged on for nearly two decades of ruinous warfare, and by the time it was finished the peninsula was devastated, its population and prosperity shattered for generations: a reminder that even a successful campaign has costs that must be weighed against its gains.

Justinian's more lasting achievement was legal, not military. He ordered a commission of jurists to collect, organize, and reconcile a thousand years of scattered Roman law into a single, coherent code, the Corpus Juris Civilis (“Body of Civil Law”), completed in 534. This code preserved Roman legal principles (contracts, property, the rights of persons) that would otherwise likely have been lost, and it would be rediscovered in medieval Italy centuries later to become the foundation of law across continental Europe. If you want a single example of how a political regime satisfies the first-order problem of “administering common laws that resolve conflicts of interest between citizens,” Justinian's Code is it.

📚 PART 2 · HAGIA SOPHIA, PLAGUE, AND WHAT CAME AFTER

After the Nika riots burned the old cathedral, Justinian rebuilt it as the Hagia Sophia, completed in 537 with the largest dome the world had yet seen: a building meant to declare, physically, that Roman and Christian grandeur had not died with the West. It remains one of the great achievements of world architecture.

Yet in 541, a devastating outbreak now known as the Plague of Justinian (likely an early strain of the same bacterium behind the later Black Death) killed perhaps a quarter of the empire's population within a few years, crippling tax revenue and manpower right as the Italian war ground on. This is one of several long-term causes of Byzantine decline that the standards ask you to understand, alongside factors you will meet later this quarter and next: renewed barbarian pressure, internal political turmoil, and, centuries later, the Crusades themselves. Roughly two centuries after Justinian, the empire would also be consumed by a bitter internal dispute called the Iconoclast Controversy, over whether religious images could be venerated at all: a controversy your appendix reading will let you examine directly. Despite all of this, the empire endured for another nine centuries after Justinian's death, a testament to how deep its institutional roots ran.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

Was Justinian's Byzantium “Rome continued” or “something new”? Weigh the evidence: a Latin-derived legal code, a reconquered Mediterranean (however briefly and at whatever cost), Roman titles and administration: against a Greek-speaking, plague-scarred, religiously turbulent state that would never again rule the West.
