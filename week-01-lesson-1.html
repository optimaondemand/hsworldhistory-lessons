The Gothic Storm: Alaric and the Sack of Rome
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In 410, a city that had not fallen to a foreign enemy in eight hundred years fell in three days. What actually broke, and when?

By the late fourth century, pressure was building on Rome's northern frontier that had nothing to do with Roman decadence and everything to do with events a thousand miles away. The Huns, a nomadic people out of Central Asia, were driving the Gothic peoples of the Danube frontier westward. In 376, tens of thousands of Goths, fleeing the Huns, asked the Eastern Emperor Valens for permission to cross the Danube and settle inside the empire. He granted it: and then Roman officials mismanaged the resettlement so badly, cheating the starving Goths in the grain trade, that the Goths rose in revolt. At the Battle of Adrianople in 378, they annihilated a Roman army and killed Valens himself, the first time in centuries an emperor had died fighting a foreign enemy on Roman soil.

📚 PART 1 · A FRAGILE PEACE

Adrianople did not end Rome; it began three decades of uneasy coexistence. The Goths, under a young leader named Alaric, were never fully absorbed and never fully expelled. Alaric wanted what most barbarian leaders of this era wanted: not the destruction of Rome, but recognition, land, supplies, and a permanent military command within the Roman system. He served Rome as a general more than once. What he could not get was a stable, honored place at all.

Contingency, not inevitability, drove what happened next. In 408, the Western Empire's most capable general, Stilicho (himself half-Vandal, and the man most likely to have kept Alaric in check through a mix of diplomacy and force) was executed on the orders of the suspicious young Emperor Honorius. With Stilicho gone, the West had no general of comparable skill and no strategy beyond stalling. Alaric marched on Rome three times in three years. On 24 August, 410, after his third siege, a gate was opened from within the city, and the Visigoths entered.

📚 PART 2 · A SACK UNLIKE THE OTHERS

What followed shocked the ancient world less for its violence than for the fact that it happened at all. Rome had not been taken by a foreign army since the Gauls under Brennus, some eight hundred years earlier. Saint Jerome, working on his translation of Scripture in Bethlehem, wrote that his voice failed him with sobs when the news reached him: “The City which had taken the whole world was itself taken.”

Yet by the standards of ancient siege warfare, Alaric's sack was restrained. Alaric was himself a Christian (an Arian Christian, of a different theological stripe than most Romans, but a Christian nonetheless), and he ordered that anyone who took refuge in the city's churches, particularly Saint Peter's Basilica, be spared and their property left untouched. Looting and burning did occur, and the psychological blow to Roman confidence was immense: but the massacre some feared did not fully materialize. This restraint is not a minor detail. It is the reason Augustine of Hippo, writing a few years later, could argue that Rome's own God, not her legions, had shown her more mercy than she deserved. You will read his argument directly tomorrow.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

It is tempting to read 410 as the moment "Rome fell." It was not; the Western imperial government would sputter on for another 66 years, and the Eastern half would outlast it by a thousand. But 410 is the moment the illusion of Roman invulnerability broke, and it shows you the kind of causation this course asks you to look for: not a grand law of historical decline, but particular men (Valens, Alaric, Stilicho, Honorius) making particular decisions, any one of which could plausibly have gone differently.
