💡 BEFORE YOU READ

Francesco di Giorgio Martini, a 15th-century Italian architect and engineer, wrote extensively on fortification design adapted specifically to withstand the new threat of gunpowder artillery, helping pioneer the low, angled, thick-walled star-shaped fortress style that would dominate European military architecture for the next three centuries, including through the Thirty Years War.

📜 PRIMARY SOURCE
Treatise on Architecture, Engineering, and Military Art (excerpted and lightly adapted)
Scholarly translation of a late 15th-century Italian manuscript; excerpt paraphrased for classroom use.
“Because the fury of bombards and other fire-engines has changed the manner of fortification which the ancients used, walls must now be built low and thick, with earth behind them to absorb the force of the shot, and towers must give way to angled bastions, so that no wall may be approached without being seen and struck from another point… for the old high towers, once the pride of a city's defense, are now its surest ruin against the new artillery.”
📌 A NOTE FOR YOU

Compare this passage directly to Constantinople's tall, thin medieval walls (Week 13), which Ottoman cannon eventually breached. Martini is describing the deliberate engineering response to exactly that kind of vulnerability: and the vastly greater cost, in money, labor, and stone, that the gunpowder age now demanded of any state wishing to defend itself.
