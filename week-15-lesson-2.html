Week 15 · Lesson 2
Worms, Geneva, and Zurich: The Reformation Spreads and Divides
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Within a decade of the Ninety-Five Theses, the Reformation had already split into rival movements, each convinced the others had gone wrong.

Summoned before the Holy Roman Emperor Charles V at the Diet of Worms in 1521 and ordered to recant his writings, Luther refused, reportedly declaring, “Here I stand, I can do no other.” Declared an outlaw by the emperor, Luther survived only because his own prince, Frederick the Wise of Saxony, sheltered him and gave him the security to translate the New Testament into German, itself a landmark achievement that helped standardize the German language and put Scripture directly into the hands of ordinary literate believers.

📚 PART 1 · ZURICH AND GENEVA

Luther's reformation was not the only one. In Zurich, the priest Huldrych Zwingli independently arrived at similar reforming conclusions around the same time, though the two reformers later split sharply over the theological meaning of the Eucharist, a reminder that “the Reformation” was never one single unified movement but a family of related, sometimes bitterly disputed, reform efforts. In Geneva, the French reformer John Calvin built a more systematically organized reformed theology, centered on the absolute sovereignty of God and the doctrine of predestination (the belief that God has already determined who will be saved), which you will read directly in today's primary source. Calvin's Geneva became a model “reformed city,” with church and civil government closely intertwined in enforcing both doctrine and public morality: its own answer, distinct from Luther's, to the church-state question this course has traced since the Investiture Controversy in Module 4.

📚 PART 2 · THE RADICAL REFORMATION AND ITS DANGERS

A further, more radical wing of reform, generally called the Anabaptists for their insistence on adult rather than infant baptism, rejected not only Catholic authority but much of the new Protestant church-state order Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin were building. Standard W.4.8 specifically names John of Leyden, a radical Anabaptist leader who, in 1534, helped seize control of the German city of Münster and proclaimed it the New Jerusalem, instituting communal property and, controversially, polygamy, under an increasingly apocalyptic and authoritarian rule. The Münster Rebellion ended in 1535 with a brutal joint Catholic-Protestant siege and mass executions, a sobering, cautionary case study in how a reforming impulse, unmoored from restraint and turned toward absolute apocalyptic certainty, could produce genuine tyranny rather than genuine reform: exactly the kind of failure the Classical Republican standard this course applies would predict from a regime that could not maintain internal order or the rule of law.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

By the 1530s, Western Christendom's religious unity, already strained since the Great Schism of 1054 and the papal Great Schism of 1378, had genuinely and permanently fractured into competing Catholic, Lutheran, Reformed (Calvinist and Zwinglian), and radical movements. Next week's lesson turns to how this same upheaval played out, in a very different form, across the English Channel.
