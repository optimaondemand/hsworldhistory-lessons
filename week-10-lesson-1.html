The Mongol Storm: Genghis Khan and the Conquest of Eurasia
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In barely seventy years, a confederation of steppe herders built the largest contiguous land empire in world history, from Korea to Poland.

In 1206, a Mongol chieftain named Temujin unified the fractious, constantly feuding tribes of the Central Asian steppe under his own rule and took the title Genghis Khan, “universal ruler.” The vast, open grasslands of the steppe (standard G.2.1 asks you to notice this physical geography directly) had long produced superb horsemen and archers; what Genghis Khan added was ruthless discipline, a meritocratic military structure that promoted based on ability rather than birth or tribe, and a deliberate strategy of terror, in which cities that resisted were annihilated as a warning to others, while cities that surrendered promptly were often spared and incorporated.

📚 PART 1 · CONQUEST ON THREE FRONTS

Mongol conquest followed Genghis Khan's death in 1227 on three enormous fronts. In the east, his grandson Kublai Khan completed the conquest of China by 1279, founding the Yuan Dynasty, the first time a foreign people had ruled the whole of China. In the Middle East, Mongol forces under Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad in 1258, executing the last Abbasid caliph and effectively ending the political center of the Islamic Golden Age you studied in Module 2: a staggering blow, though Islamic learning and culture continued to flourish elsewhere, from Cairo to Granada. In the west, Mongol armies smashed a coalition of Polish and German knights at the Battle of Legnica in 1241 and stood poised to advance further into Central Europe: only to withdraw suddenly when news arrived of the death of the Great Khan Ogedei, since Mongol law required the empire's senior commanders to return home for the selection of a successor. Central and Western Europe was never invaded again. This withdrawal is one of history's clearest examples of contingency: a distant succession custom, not any European military victory, spared Western Europe from further Mongol conquest.

QUICK CHECK-IN
The Mongols withdrew from Central Europe not because of a European victory, but because of a succession custom back home. What does this tell you about how to explain major historical turning points?
📚 PART 2 · THE PAX MONGOLICA

Whatever their brutality in conquest, the Mongols governed their vast empire with striking religious tolerance and a genuine interest in trade and communication across it. The relative security of Mongol rule across Central Asia, sometimes called the Pax Mongolica (“Mongol peace”), reopened and protected the overland Silk Road trade routes between China and Europe on a scale not seen in centuries, allowing merchants, missionaries, and travelers (including the Venetian merchant Marco Polo, whom you will read tomorrow) to cross the length of Eurasia with unprecedented (though still real) safety.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

The Mongols connected civilizations that had rarely had direct contact, for better (trade, the exchange of ideas and technology) and for worse, as you will see later this course when the same trade routes likely carried the Black Death itself westward. Tomorrow's lesson turns to the one major target the Mongols could not conquer: Japan.
