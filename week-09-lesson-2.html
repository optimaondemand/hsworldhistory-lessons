The Kingdom of Jerusalem: From Godfrey to the Sack of Constantinople
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The Crusader States survived less than two centuries. Along the way, one crusade never even reached the Holy Land, and instead sacked the greatest Christian city in the world.

Godfrey of Bouillon died within a year of Jerusalem's capture, and his brother Baldwin I became the first to actually take the title King of Jerusalem in 1100. Under Baldwin and his successors, four Crusader States took shape along the eastern Mediterranean coast: the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the County of Tripoli, the Principality of Antioch, and the County of Edessa. All four were perpetually undermanned frontier societies, dependent on a thin trickle of new crusaders and settlers from Europe, and on new military-religious orders, the Knights Templar (founded 1119) and the Knights Hospitaller, who combined monastic vows with permanent professional soldiering to help garrison the frontier.

📚 PART 1 · SALADIN AND THE THIRD CRUSADE

The Crusader States' fortunes depended heavily on Muslim disunity persisting; when the brilliant general Saladin unified Egypt and Syria under his own rule in the 1170s and 1180s, that advantage disappeared. Saladin crushed the kingdom's army at the Battle of Hattin in 1187 and recaptured Jerusalem itself later that year, ending 88 years of Crusader rule over the city. The resulting Third Crusade (1189–1192), featuring England's King Richard the Lionheart, recovered some coastal territory and negotiated Christian pilgrim access to Jerusalem, but could not retake the city itself. The Kingdom of Jerusalem survived afterward only as a much smaller coastal remnant.

📚 PART 2 · THE FOURTH CRUSADE: A DISASTER FOR CHRISTENDOM ITSELF

The final, strangest turn in this story came in 1204. The Fourth Crusade, meant to strike at Muslim-held Egypt, was diverted by a combination of debt to Venetian ship-financiers, a Byzantine succession dispute, and simple opportunism, into an attack on Constantinople itself: the greatest Christian city in the world, and the same city whose emperor's appeal for help had helped launch the whole crusading movement a century earlier. The crusaders sacked the city with a thoroughness and brutality that shocked contemporaries on all sides, looting relics, treasures, and even bronze statues, and installed a short-lived Latin Empire in Constantinople's place.

This is a crucial piece of evidence for the causes of Byzantine decline you first encountered in Week 2: alongside Justinian's Plague and centuries of external pressure, the Fourth Crusade shows fellow Christians dealing Byzantium one of the most devastating blows in its entire history. The Byzantines eventually recaptured Constantinople in 1261, but the empire never fully recovered its former strength: a genuinely unintended, deeply contingent consequence with enormous long-term effects, and a sober reminder that a movement launched in the name of Christian solidarity could end by nearly destroying the very Christian empire it first set out to rescue.

💪 WHY IT MATTERS

The Crusades began as an answer to a Byzantine emperor's plea and ended, a century later, with a Christian army sacking that same emperor's capital. Few historical arcs illustrate contingency, and the gap between intention and outcome, more starkly. This week's VR Activity asks you to bring this whole arc, and the eight exhibits preceding it, together into a single argument.
